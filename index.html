Pleistocene ice ages

Changes in

  • Ice cover
  • Sea level

  • Ocean gateways
Since the last ice age period started, temperature swung in the Pleistocene (a geological epoch) from warm to cool conditions in a regular pattern. The LGM (started 20,000 ya). At this time, glaciers covered the northern hemisphere and trapped so much sea water that the sea level was about 130m lower than nowadays. Moreover ocean currents changed and ocean gateways to the Arctic closed. Oxygen-isotope method. 18O/16O isotope ratios depend on temperature. Its ratio in fossil shells thus indicates the temperatures of the water they existed in. In the deep sea it was due to glacier coverage. 16O evaporated more easily in glaciers and left a higher proportion of 18O in the seas.

Carbon sequestration (61-268 Tg C yr-1)

Krause-Jensen & Duarte (2016); Nature Geoscience

Air bladders are common among brown algal taxa and facilitate their long-range transport (i). Langmuir circulation forms windrows of macroalgae (ii) and can force the algae to depths where water pressure makes the air bladders burst and the algae then sink. Macroalgal carbon can be sequestered either via burial in the habitat or by transport to the deep sea where it is sequestered whether buried or not (iii). About 90% of this sequestration occurs through export to the deep sea, and the rest through burial in coastal sediments.

Which climate change related factor affects ocean forests most?

Climate change related factors

  • Salinity
  • pH
  • Temperature
Salinity increases in some regions and decreases in others. PH decreases on average due to the reactions that take place when CO2 dissolves in ocean water. Hydrogen ions are released and this makes the water more acidic.

Identifying most important range-limiting factors

We wanted to identify out of a range of environmental factors which of them are most important for the distribution of seaweed. Because we wanted to know how the distribution of a range of macroalgae will change under projected climate change. We did this with an approach called ecological niche modelling. Here the model allows to evaluate out of a range of environmental factors, which one can predict the presence and absence of the species best.

Temperature as most important factor

Jueterbock et al. (2013,2016); Ecology and Evolution

For three of the dominant temperate species and the most dominant intertidal species in the arctic, sea surface temperature and surface air temperature derivates were the most important factors. This was later confirmed for several other species, including subtidal kelp species and seagrasses.

Seaweed distribution limited by isotherms

Diversity highest between 10°C summer and 20°C winter isotherm
Over large spatial scales, the distribution of seaweeds is generally limited by certain isotherms (line of equal temperature on a geographic map) to the north and the south. This could be lethal temperatures for adult plants but most often it is the temperature that does not allow for successful reproduction or recruitment. Early life stages are generally the most sensitive life stage in marine macrophytes. Many seaweeds are limited by the 10°C summer and the 20°C winter isotherms, which means that this temperate region is the most diverse in terms of number of seaweed species.

Dispersal and invasive potential

Poleward range extension depends on the ability of the species to disperse and migrate. Since zygotes generally disperse less than 10 m from the egg-bearing female, long-range dispersal thus must involve drifting thalli of reproductively mature individuals. Some species have air bladders that allow them to drift. For the others, shipping transport will likely play a major role. As shipping has increased in the Canadian and Russian Arctic in response to the loss of Arctic sea ice, it is likely to play a major role in the introduction of marine species into polar areas, including marine macroalgae.

Shifts from kelp forests to sediment-laden turf

Flattending diversity

Filbee-Dexter & Wernberg (2017), BioScience

In several regions around the globe, including Norway, kelp forests are currently being replaced by turf algae. There are several drivers for such a regime shift, including gradual warming, and heatwaves. Turf algae are smaller algae with densely packed branches and small filaments that grow fast, and cover the sea floor like a carpet.
Turf-covered reefs are totally lacking the three-dimensional structure of a kelp forest, where an immense richness of crustaceans, fish and snails live and hide. In contrast to sea urchin barrens, where kelp can easily re-establish once the urchins are removed, there are mechanisms in the turf algae ecosystem that protects from reestablishment.

Hyndes et al. (2016), BioScience

Initially, we expect tropical herbivorous fishes to establish in temperate seagrass meadows, followed later by megafauna. Tropical seagrasses are likely to establish later, delayed by more limited dispersal abilities. Ultimately, food webs are likely to shift from primarily seagrass-detritus to more direct- consumption-based systems, thereby affecting a range of important ecosystem services that seagrasses provide, including their nursery habitat role for fishery species, carbon sequestration, and the provision of organic matter to other ecosystems in temperate regions. Increasing herbivory is particularly impacting in the winter months when light availability is limited. Green turtles already occur in small numbers in the temperate west coast of Australia. Ultimately, we expect this detrital-based food web to shift to one that is based more on direct consumption of seagrass.
